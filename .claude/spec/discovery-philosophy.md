# Discovery Philosophy

How the hypothesis pipeline discovers new operations.

- **include:** discovery framing, horizon definitions

## Core Principle

The library is a standard library for people's lives. Its operations support people in activities — working, creating, deciding, learning, organizing, collaborating, reflecting. The activities are the point.

`score` is the archetype of a great operation. It is simultaneously a word everyone knows, an abstract mechanism that works across all domains via its instruction parameter, a composable primitive with clear I/O, and a building block that drives hundreds of applications its designer never anticipated. These qualities aren't separate — they're facets of the same thing. An operation that's genuinely abstract and genuinely useful will have all of them naturally.

Discovery finds the next operations like `score`. It uses futures thinking, design thinking, and cross-domain pattern recognition as generative lenses, but the output is always operations that support people in real activities across many contexts.

---

## Three Discovery Horizons

Each horizon approaches the same question — what operations would support people in their activities? — from a different starting point. Together they ensure the pipeline considers diverse sources of inspiration without collapsing into any single frame.

### H1 — Technical Patterns Elevated to Meaning

Programming, mathematics, and systems thinking contain patterns that turn out to be universal when applied to meaning. `map`, `filter`, `reduce`, `sort` came from programming but support activities far beyond code. What other technical patterns would be equally universal at the semantic level?

### H2 — Activities People Already Do

People evaluate, compare, categorize, extract, and sort — and those are already operations. What other activities do people do across many contexts that have a consistent shape and would be just as powerful as composable functions? The richest source is activities that span many different domains of life.

### H3 — Functions Hiding in Other Fields

Cognitive science, linguistics, decision science, creativity research, and other fields describe structured processes that people engage in. Some of these map cleanly to composable operations with clear I/O. The source is the field's insight into human activity, but the output is an intuitive operation named in plain language.
