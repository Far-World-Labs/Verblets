---
name: decompose
description: Find and carry out decomposition improvements that make the project's real structure easier to see and work with. Use when responsibilities are tangled, boundaries are blurred, or the visible arrangement no longer matches the meaningful arrangement.
argument-hint: "[focus area or shaping direction]"
---

Work to improve the project by finding and carrying out decomposition improvements that make the real structure easier to see and easier to work with. If a focus or shaping direction is given, use it as your point of entry; if not, range widely enough to find the places where the project is ready for a truer shape.

Think in terms of burden, center, and movement. Look for places where too much is being asked of one surface, where one idea has been spread thinly across many surfaces, where behavior depends on pieces that cannot be understood together, where power is present but hard to see, where change must travel too far to remain safe, and where the visible arrangement of the project no longer matches the meaningful arrangement. Also look for the quieter failure: places that appear messy only because they hold a real unity that should be strengthened instead of split.

Bring the strengths that agents have and humans do not. Compare across distance. Notice structural echoes. Track how the same concept appears under different names, how one workflow is distributed across code, prompts, context, and effects, how one responsibility leaks across boundaries, how one local decision creates confusion elsewhere, how one good pattern fails to propagate, how one weak pattern keeps multiplying. Let the wider field inform the local move.

A strong decomposition gives important things a place to live. It gives reasoning a cleaner center, puts effects where they can be seen and governed, keeps behaviorally coupled pieces within reach of one another, gathers scenarios and constraints nearer to the logic they shape, and reduces the amount of reconstruction required to understand what is going on. It should make the project feel more direct, more stable in its promises, and more capable of being changed without guesswork.

Do not decompose for neatness. Do not abstract because repetition exists. Do not split because a file is large or merge because two names sound related. Follow the deeper joints of the work. Preserve density when it carries real meaning. Preserve convenience when it genuinely lowers cognitive strain. Make sharper separations where mixed concerns blur risk or purpose. Make stronger unities where fragmentation has hidden the actual center.

Carry out improvements with enough breadth that the result feels intentional rather than patched. Let the change teach the right lesson about the system. Make the project easier to read through structure, easier to trust through clearer boundaries, and easier to evolve because the next move can be seen from where you leave it.

If the area is already well-decomposed, or if you find tensions but cannot confidently resolve them, do not invent work. Report what you examined and what you found — including places that are healthy and why. Propose decomposition moves you considered but weren't sure about, with enough detail that the user can judge whether they're worth pursuing. Honest assessment of near-misses and trade-offs is more valuable than forced restructuring.

Verify all changes with tests and build before reporting results.
