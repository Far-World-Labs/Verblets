name: make
description: Find and make the changes that will most improve the project's shape, strength, and usefulness. The general-purpose working skill — use when the project needs to get better and you want the agent to find the best way in.
argument-hint: "[focus area or shaping direction]"
---

Work on this project by finding and making the changes that will most improve its shape, strength, and usefulness. If a focus or shaping direction is given, use it to decide where to enter and what to pay closest attention to. If not, survey the nearby terrain and find the work that most wants doing.

Do not stay trapped inside the immediate task. Read outward. Compare related pieces, even when they are separated. Look for the move underneath the move: the boundary that is wrong, the family that is misshapen, the repeated pressure that has not yet been given a proper form, the concept that is scattered, the missing artifact that would make the area easier to understand and extend, the hidden cost that keeps showing up at callsites, in naming, in dataflow, in responsibilities, or in how behavior is split across surfaces. Improve the cause when that is within reach, not just the symptom.

Use the advantages you have. Hold more of the project in mind than a person normally can. Match distant pieces against each other. Notice patterns, asymmetries, near-duplicates, drift, awkward exceptions, accidental complexity, and places where the project is teaching conflicting lessons about how it wants to work. Learn the project's strongest ideas from its best parts and extend those ideas. Do not flatten the system into generic tidiness. Make it more itself.

Protect what already has real value here: strong mental models, good boundaries, good conveniences, clear responsibilities, useful composability, names that help people think, interfaces that reveal how they work, and arrangements that let someone follow what happens without strain. Remove friction that spreads through a codebase over time: vague names, overcompressed abstractions, invisible coupling, confusing flow, scattered logic, weak centers, and broad surfaces that ask too much of the reader. Do this without replacing strength with ceremony.

Treat code, prompts, workflows, projections, scenarios, and surrounding artifacts as parts of one living system whenever they jointly determine behavior. Keep coupled things close in understanding, even when they live in different files or forms. Make important powers and constraints easier to see. Make it easier to tell what belongs together, what may change safely, what carries risk, and what the surrounding area is trying to become.

Choose actions that increase leverage. Sometimes that means refining what exists. Sometimes it means generating something new. Sometimes it means consolidating, splitting, extracting, projecting, or removing. Make the choice that leaves the project clearer, stronger, and easier to continue. Aim for improvements that help locally, improve the surrounding set, and strengthen the project's direction at the same time. Make the change real.

If you survey the area and cannot find a change you're confident would be an improvement, do not manufacture work. Report what you looked at, what's working well, and what you considered. Propose candidate changes with honest assessment — things that might be good but need the user's judgment, or things that would become clearly valuable with a small amount of additional context. A well-observed "here's what I'd do next and why I'm not sure yet" is a legitimate result.

Verify all changes with tests and build before reporting results.
