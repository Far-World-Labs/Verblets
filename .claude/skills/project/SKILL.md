Use when the user wants to surface hidden structure, reveal how a family of code relates, expose a boundary or pattern that needs a clear form, or give humans or machines a stronger handle on part of the system."
argument-hint: "[focus area or shaping direction]"
---

Generate a projection that helps this project see itself and move more intelligently.

Work from whatever focus or shaping direction is given. If the instruction points at a particular area, enter there, but do not stay trapped at that surface if the real shape extends beyond it. If no focus is given, look for the place where a new or improved projection would create the most leverage: a place where related things are being felt separately, where an important pattern has no clear form yet, where future edits are being steered by weak or partial understanding, or where the project is close to becoming more coherent, more powerful, or more navigable.

A projection is not a summary and not a dump of observations. It is a crafted view that helps something important become perceivable enough to guide action. It may gather a family into one view, reveal a boundary as a real boundary instead of a folder line, show how behavior is distributed across code and adjacent artifacts, make a hidden invariant visible, expose drag on human reasoning, surface a power structure, or give machines a cleaner handle on a part of the system that is currently awkward to reason over. Choose the kind of projection that gives the strongest new sight.

Begin by looking for existing projections, explicit or implied. Treat them as previous attempts to hold onto something that mattered. Decide whether to continue one, sharpen one, merge several, split one apart, or replace one that has started to mislead. Even when nothing formal exists, assume there is already some working projection embedded in the current arrangement of code, notes, naming, workflows, or habits of change. Find that before you decide what to do next.

Use the advantages you have that humans do not. Keep many related pieces present at once. Compare across distance without losing local detail. See families, asymmetries, near-duplicates, warped interfaces, scattered responsibility, silent coupling, inconsistent teaching in naming or structure, and places where one good idea appears in several partial forms. At the same time, stay close to what humans pay for: uncertainty at callsites, hard-to-follow data movement, invisible powers, blurred boundaries, and surfaces that require too much reconstruction before a person can act with confidence.

Let the project teach you how to project it. Look for its best internal language, its best mental models, its most powerful conveniences, its strongest pure cores, its real boundaries, its recurring scenario shapes, its live invariants, its most meaningful distinctions. Build the projection out of those forces so it feels native to the project rather than imposed from outside. Strengthen what is real. Remove what is accidental. Make it easier to predict one part by knowing another, without forcing unlike things into false sameness.

Treat projections as consequential edits to the project's field of understanding. They can interrupt current work, erase useful ambiguity, harden the wrong model, or destroy continuity that people were relying on. Move thoughtfully. Preserve continuity where it carries judgment, history, or practical navigation. Break continuity where the old shape is quietly weakening the project. The point is not tidiness. The point is stronger sight and better motion.

Choose the audience deliberately. A projection may be for human use, machine use, or both. If that is not specified, decide based on which audience would benefit most right now and say what you chose. Then make the projection serve that audience fully rather than hedging into vagueness.

Store projections in `.claude/projections/` named for their scope. When updating an existing projection, your new version replaces it entirely.

If you cannot find a projection that would create real new sight, say so and explain what you looked at. Describe the projections that already exist (or are implied by the current arrangement), assess their health, and propose what projection would become valuable next — even if you aren't ready to build it yet. A clear account of what the project can already see and where its vision is weakest is itself useful output.

Respond by saying what projection you found or made and how this run relates to what was already there. Say who it is for. Present the projection as the central result. Close by saying what it newly allows the project to see, compare, protect, or change.
